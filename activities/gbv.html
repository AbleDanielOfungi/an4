Gender-based Violence (GBV) in Uganda is a significant human rights issue affecting various demographics, especially women and girls. It manifests in different forms, including domestic violence, sexual harassment, female genital mutilation (FGM), child marriages, and emotional abuse.

Domestic Violence: According to the Uganda Demographic and Health Survey (UDHS) 2016, 51% of ever-married women aged 15-49 have experienced spousal violence. Sexual Violence: About 22% of women have experienced sexual violence at some point in their lives. Child Marriages: Uganda has one of the highest child marriage rates in the world, with 40% of girls married before the age of 18.

Forms of Gender-Based Violence

Physical Violence: Most common form, involving beating, choking, or other forms of physical assault.

Sexual Violence: Includes rape, defilement, and forced sex within marriages or relationships.

Emotional and Psychological Abuse: Verbal insults, threats, and humiliation.

Economic Violence: Denial of resources, opportunities, or finances by an intimate partner.

Female Genital Mutilation (FGM): Still practiced in some Ugandan communities, particularly in the Karamoja and Sebei regions, despite its prohibition.

Contributing Factors

Cultural Norms: Patriarchal attitudes that view women as subordinate to men contribute to widespread GBV.

Poverty: Financial dependence on men forces many women to stay in abusive relationships.

Weak Legal Enforcement: Although laws like the Domestic Violence Act (2010) and the Anti-FGM Act (2010) exist, enforcement is often weak due to inadequate resources and patriarchal legal systems.

Lack of Awareness: Many women, especially in rural areas, are unaware of their rights and do not know where to seek help.

Response and Support Systems

Shelters: Few shelters exist for survivors of GBV, and they are often underfunded.

Hotlines: Uganda has GBV helplines, but coverage is limited.

NGOs and Civil Society Organizations: Groups like ActionAid, the Uganda Women's Network (UWONET), and FIDA Uganda provide legal, psychosocial, and shelter support to survivors.

Our Community Interventions

Community Dialogues: Engaging men, traditional leaders, and religious groups in dialogues to address harmful norms that perpetuate GBV.

Education Campaigns: Raising awareness about GBV laws and women's rights through radio, TV, and community outreach programs.

Challenges in Addressing GBV

Underreporting: Due to stigma, fear of reprisal, and lack of trust in law enforcement, many cases of GBV go unreported.

Corruption: Some law enforcement officials are bribed, leading to the dismissal or weakening of cases.

Cultural Resistance: Some cultural norms still view GBV as a private, family issue, making community members reluctant to intervene.

Recommendations

Strengthening Law Enforcement: Provide training to police and judiciary officials to handle GBV cases more effectively.

Empowering Women Economically: Supporting women with skills and resources to reduce economic dependence on abusive partners.

Increased Funding for GBV Programs: More resources should be allocated to shelters, legal aid, and psychosocial support for survivors.

Community Involvement: Encourage men and boys to take part in campaigns to challenge patriarchal norms and promote gender equality.
