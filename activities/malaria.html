Support for malaria prevention, diagnosis, and treatment in the context of the Covid-19 pandemic

  • Dr Giovanni Dall’Oglio CUAMM Area PM
  • Oyam and Kole District

Malaria is a major public health concern in Uganda, where it remains one of the leading causes of illness and death. Despite efforts to combat the disease, it continues to affect millions of people annually, particularly vulnerable populations such as children under five and pregnant women.

Malaria is a leading cause of mortality in Uganda, in 2022 responsible for 31.1% of all out-patient attendance, 25% of all inpatient admissions and 7.4% of all deaths in Uganda health facilities.

7.4% of all deaths in Uganda health facilities.

Wide regional disparity exists within the country in relation to the Malaria Prevalence in Children under 5 years with Lango registering 13%, but less compared Karamoja (34%), West Nile (22%) and Busoga (21%). The Malaria prevalence in Uganda mirrors the poverty map, poor areas also tend to have poor access to health services and have poor health-seeking behavior.

Malaria Situation-Epidemiology

WHO: Low malaria transmission zone < 250 cases per 1,000 person Moderate malaria transmission zone: incidence between 250 to 450 cases per 1000 person (or parasite prevalence between 10–35%); High malaria transmission zone: incidence > 450 cases per 1000 person (or parasite prevalence > 35%).

ERASE PROJECT- Specific objectives and expected outcomes

General Objective

To contribute to the reduction of annual deaths and morbidity due to malaria in Lango sub-region, in line with United Nations Sustainable Development Goals 3.3 . and Ministry of Health Uganda Malaria Reduction and Elimination Strategic Plan (UMRESP) 2021-2025

Specific objective

To increase access to malaria prevention and curative quality services by the community, especially women and children, in Oyam and Kole districts.

Expected Outcomes

Malaria Prevention including vector control is reinforced in the districts of intervention thanks to the engagement of the entire community (VHT, schools, community leaders, and organized groups), leading to an increased number of people adopting positive preventive behaviors.

The quality of Malaria case management is improved in the health facilities and communities of intervention

Evidence-based analysis and coordination of response against malaria at district level is strengthened – (Improve the quality of screening, treatment and follow-up services, including surveillance and response to malaria foci).

Activity Implementation Output

1.1 Providing support to VHTs in promoting the concept of Malaria Smart Homes

Through 300 VHTs, 4,372 Malaria smart homes, (1665 in Kole and 2,707 in Oyam), have been identified and received the certificate.

6,193 lemon grass stems distributed by VHT coordinators for planting and redistribution to households (promotion of local mosquito repellant plants), 3,023 in Kole, and 3,169 in Oyam.

An assessment has been done in 2,464 HHs in the project sub-counties of Oyam District to analyze the effectiveness of implementing smart home prevention strategy on reduction of malaria incidence on children under 5 years old, from Oct to Dec 2023.

1.2: Strengthening school committees in malaria education and awareness activities

Despite a target of 4,500, 17,262 pupils, (8,778 in Oyam and 8,484 in Kole) have been sensitized during school debates and drama sessions with malaria messages; 791 teachers, ( 349 in Oyam and 442 in Kole), were reached through sensitization during school debates and drama sessions with malaria messages;


Causes

Malaria is caused by the Plasmodium parasite, transmitted to humans through the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. The most common and dangerous strain in Uganda is Plasmodium falciparum, which can cause severe and life-threatening symptoms.

Symptoms

Symptoms of malaria include fever, chills, headache, muscle aches, and fatigue. If untreated, it can lead to severe complications like anemia, organ failure, and death.

Control and Prevention Efforts

Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs): Distributed nationwide to protect people from mosquito bites while they sleep.

Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS): Spraying insecticide inside homes to kill mosquitoes and reduce transmission.

Antimalarial drugs: Both preventive measures and treatment regimens are in place, including artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs), which are the standard treatment for Plasmodium falciparum malaria.

Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs): These are used for the quick identification of malaria, improving treatment accuracy.

Government and International Support:

Uganda's Ministry of Health, in collaboration with global health organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO), the U.S. President’s Malaria Initiative (PMI), and the Global Fund, continues to roll out large-scale malaria control programs.

Challenges

Resistance to insecticides and drugs: There have been growing concerns about mosquitoes becoming resistant to common insecticides and the parasite becoming resistant to some antimalarial drugs.

Funding and logistics: Although many bed nets and medical supplies are provided, logistical issues can hamper distribution, especially in remote areas.

Climate factors: Uganda’s tropical climate and abundant rainfall create favorable breeding conditions for mosquitoes, making malaria a year-round threat in most regions.
