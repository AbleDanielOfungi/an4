HIV/AIDS in Uganda: An Overview

  • Dr Giovanni Dall’Oglio CUAMM Area PM
  • Oyam and Kole District

Uganda has long been recognized as a global leader in the fight against HIV/AIDS, having been one of the first countries in sub-Saharan Africa to experience the epidemic. Over the years, Uganda has made significant progress in reducing HIV prevalence through a combination of strong political leadership, community-based initiatives, and comprehensive public health campaigns.

HIV/AIDS Prevalence

As of recent reports, Uganda's HIV prevalence rate stands at around 5.4% among adults aged 15-49. Despite this progress, HIV continues to be a major public health concern, with over 1.4 million people living with HIV in the country. New infections still occur, with about 53,000 new HIV infections recorded annually.

Uganda's response to the HIV/AIDS epidemic has been largely driven by a multi-sectoral approach that involves government agencies, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and international partners

7.4% of all deaths in Uganda health facilities.

More recently, Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) has become widely available through government health centers and partnerships with international organizations, contributing significantly to reducing HIV-related deaths and mother-to-child transmission rates.

Challenges in HIV/AIDS Management

Stigma and Discrimination: Many people living with HIV/AIDS still face stigma in their communities, which hinders testing, treatment adherence, and public awareness.

Youth Vulnerability: Adolescents and young adults (15-24 years) are particularly vulnerable, accounting for a significant portion of new infections.

Gender Inequality: Women in Uganda are disproportionately affected by HIV/AIDS, with higher prevalence rates than men. Social and economic vulnerabilities contribute to their increased risk of infection.

Funding and Resources: There is a continued need for financial and infrastructural support to sustain ART programs, prevention efforts, and healthcare facilities in rural areas.

Future Outlook

The government of Uganda, in collaboration with international partners, has set ambitious goals for ending AIDS by 2030. These include efforts to increase access to ART, enhance HIV testing and counseling services, and integrate HIV prevention into sexual and reproductive health services. Key areas of focus moving forward include youth-targeted interventions, promoting safe sexual practices, expanding voluntary medical male circumcision, and scaling up the use of Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP) for high-risk populations.

Conclusion

While Uganda has made significant strides in its fight against HIV/AIDS, continued efforts are needed to sustain progress and address emerging challenges. Strengthening health systems, reducing stigma, and improving access to education and healthcare for vulnerable populations are critical steps in controlling the epidemic and achieving the national goal of eliminating HIV/AIDS as a public health threat by 2030.

Our HIV/AIDS Intervention

• Promote HIV/AIDS prevention among Orphans and Vulnerable Children (OVCs). • Address stigma, discrimination, and/or gender-based violence (GBV). • Promote HIV/AIDS care and treatment and adherence to treatment. • Fight against HIV/AIDS among Adolescents Girls and Young Women (AGYW). • Use Faith-Based Community approaches to combat HIV/AIDS • Support People Living with HIV (PLHIV) with food relief • Strengthen the ability of communities to identify needs and to develop and implement activities focused on HIV/AIDS prevention, care, and support for OVC and persons living with HIV and AIDS (PLHA). • Support HIV/AIDS People with Disabilities (PWDs) • Screening and testing for HIV/TB and TB-related morbidity e.g.HIV counseling and testing including Boda-Boda stages and Truck Drivers. • Provide HIV/AIDs information services and BCC to enhance community knowledge and motivate them to change behavior practices and reduce their vulnerability over • Increase the levels of community awareness, knowledge, and risk perception on STI and HIV amongst truckers.

• Provide care facilities for STI infections and counseling • Promote the consistent and correct use of condoms among truck drivers and communities. • Referral campaigns of truck drivers for diagnosis of TB and HIV • Training of HIV/AIDS peer support and education and individual follow-up. • Advocacy on Social support e.g. food supplementation. • Conducting households through a door-to-door approach which provides privacy for taking sputum samples, and conducting RDTs and couple counseling. • Conducting drama on HIV/AIDS prevention knowledge on culture and practices. • Training of young people through a process of entertainment that includes training and supporting them with incentives to perform street theatre with HIV and TB messages. • Conducting HIV/AIDS sports as a means for motivation and information dissemination, including netball focused on young women and football for young men. • Conduct sensitization to involve local NGOs, CBOS, and all partners in the existing network of positive people and improve their capacity through their participation in training workshops and community sensitization meetings.
